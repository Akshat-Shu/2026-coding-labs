Assignment 1: Marriage Pact — short answers
=============================================

Q1. reserve vs resize — and the capacity you chose in get_applicants.
    YOUR ANSWER: reserved a capacity of 1000 in order to avoid resizing.
    We already have an idea of the total number of applicants, so we can 
    reserve that much space to avoid resizing overhead while calling push_back repeatedly.


Q2. What does it mean for a std::ranges algorithm to take a projection?
    Give one example from your find_matches (or explain why a projection
    didn't help).
    YOUR ANSWER: A projection transforms the elements of the container before 
    they are passed to the algorithm, which in this case was an equality comparison.
    In find_matches, we used a projection to transform the student names into their initials.
    An alternative way to do the same thing would be to apply the function inside the lambda,
    using a projection allows us to separate the transformation logic from the comparison logic.


Q3. Iterator invalidation in run_mixer. Which strategy did you choose
    (erase-returns-next-iterator / indices / restart) and why?
    What specifically goes wrong with the naive approach?
    YOUR ANSWER: I chose the erase-returns-next-iterator strategy because it allows us to safely 
    remove elements without bothering about out-of-bounds access and this approach is more efficient
    in comparison to restarting.
    The primary issue with the naive approach is iterator invalidation, once we delete the current
    element as well as the matching element, the iterators pointing to those elements become invalid
    and accessing them would lead to undefined behavior.
    When an iterator is erased, the iterators before the erased element remain valid 
    and can be safely used to access the elements at those positions.
    The Naive approach is less efficient because it requires us to find matches at each iteration 
    of the loop, only to find a single matching pair, an optimization over this would be to first
    bucket all of the applicants by their initials and then we can just iterate through the 
    buckets to find matches. 


Q4. at() vs []. When, if ever, would you reach for vec.at(i) over
    vec[i] in this assignment?
    YOUR ANSWER: Inside the function initials, we have to get the first characters 
    of the first and last name, the braced accessor leads to undefined behaviour
    if the index is out-of-bounds. On the other hand, .at() would throw an error 
    instead if index is invalid.
